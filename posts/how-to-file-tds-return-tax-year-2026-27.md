## 1. The One Rule That Decides Old Act vs New Act

Before anything else, understand this. It governs everything else in this guide.

> **The trigger is the earlier of credit or payment.**
>
> * Earlier of credit or payment falls **on or before 31 March 2026** → Income Tax Act, **1961** applies. Old sections. Old forms.
> * Earlier of credit or payment falls **on or after 1 April 2026** → Income Tax Act, **2025** applies. New sections. New forms.

The date you *deposit* the tax does not change this. The date you *file* the return does not change this. Only the date of credit or payment matters.

**What this means in practice:** two Acts are running in parallel right now.

| Return                          | Period       | Governing Act | Forms to use             |
| ------------------------------- | ------------ | ------------- | ------------------------ |
| Q4 FY 2025-26 (belated/revised) | Jan–Mar 2026 | Act, 1961     | Form 24Q, 26Q, 27Q, 27EQ |
| Q1 Tax Year 2026-27             | Apr–Jun 2026 | Act, 2025     | Form 138, 140, 144, 143  |

Both may be sitting on your desk in July 2026. **Do not file a Q1 TY 2026-27 return on Form 24Q. Do not file a Q4 FY 2025-26 correction on Form 138.** This is the single most common error CPC is flagging.

## 2. Income Tax Act 1961 vs Income Tax Act 2025: What Actually Changed

The most important thing to tell your clients and your team: **this is a renumbering and restructuring, not a tax reform.**

| Basis                            | Income Tax Act, 1961 (Old)            | Income Tax Act, 2025 (New)                    |
| -------------------------------- | ------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------- |
| **Effective for TDS**            | Payments up to 31 March 2026          | Payments from 1 April 2026                    |
| **Total sections**               | ~819 sections, 14 schedules           | 536 sections, 16 schedules                    |
| **TDS sections**                 | ~60+ scattered sections (192 to 194T) | Consolidated into **392, 393, 394**           |
| **Structure of TDS law**         | Section-by-section, scattered         | Single tabular format with **Nature Codes**   |
| **Period concept**               | Previous Year + Assessment Year       | **Tax Year** (single 12-month concept)        |
| **Salary TDS**                   | Section 192                           | **Section 392**                               |
| **Non-salary TDS**               | Sections 193 to 194T                  | **Section 393**                               |
| **TCS**                          | Section 206C                          | **Section 394**                               |
| **Salary return**                | Form 24Q                              | **Form 138**                                  |
| **Non-salary return (resident)** | Form 26Q                              | **Form 140**                                  |
| **Non-resident return**          | Form 27Q                              | **Form 144**                                  |
| **TCS return**                   | Form 27EQ                             | **Form 143**                                  |
| **Salary TDS certificate**       | Form 16                               | **Form 130**                                  |
| **Employee declaration**         | Form 12BB                             | **Form 124**                                  |
| **TDS rates**                    | —                                     | **Unchanged**                                 |
| **Thresholds**                   | —                                     | **Unchanged**                                 |
| **Correction window**            | Longer                                | **2 years** from end of FY of original return |

### What did NOT change

* **TDS rates.** A contractor payment is still 1% / 2%. Professional fees are still 10%.
* **Threshold limits.** Still ₹30,000 / ₹1,00,000 for contracts, ₹50,000 for professional fees, and so on.
* **Your TAN.** No re-registration needed.
* **Due dates for TDS returns.** Still 31 July, 31 October, 31 January, 31 May.
* **Deposit due date.** Still the 7th of the following month.

### The "Tax Year" concept

Under the old Act, income earned in FY 2025-26 was assessed in AY 2026-27. Two labels for one stream of income, and endless confusion.

The 2025 Act scraps this. A **Tax Year** is a single 12-month period from 1 April to 31 March in which income is both earned and reported. Income earned from 1 April 2026 to 31 March 2027 is simply **Tax Year 2026-27**.

**Practical warning:** when depositing TDS on a challan for April 2026 onwards, select **"Tax Year 2026-27"**. Selecting "AY 2026-27" will misallocate your payment to FY 2025-26 records and create a short-payment default.

## 5. Important TDS Sections Under the New Act

### Section 392 — TDS on Salary

Covers salary, perquisites, ESOPs and EPF withdrawals. Deduction is at slab rates after considering the employee's declared deductions and chosen regime.

**Action point:** employers must **reset the TDS computation from 1 April 2026** for the new Tax Year, considering projected income, deductions and the tax regime for TY 2026-27. Investment declarations should now reference the 2025 Act — for instance, what was a Section 80C deduction is now **Schedule XV read with Section 123**.

### Section 393 — TDS on Non-Salary Payments

The workhorse section. It consolidates everything from Section 193 through 194T into a single tabular provision, organised by serial number and Nature Code. Contractors, rent, professional fees, commission, interest, dividends, e-commerce, virtual digital assets — all of it now sits here.

**Substantive change worth noting:** the ambiguity over whether **manpower supply / labour contract services** counted as "work" under old Section 194C has been resolved. The 2025 Act **explicitly includes manpower supply services** as "work" under the Section 393 equivalent.

### Section 394 — TCS

Governs Tax Collected at Source from 1 April 2026, replacing old Section 206C. Some TCS rates have been revised — notably scrap and specified minerals (up to 2%) and overseas tour packages (simplified to a flat 2%).

### Section 397 — Filing of Statements

The return-filing obligation. Form 140 is filed under Section 397 read with Rule 219 of the Income Tax Rules, 2026.

### Section 427 — Late Filing Fee

The new equivalent of old Section 234E. ₹200 per day for late furnishing of TDS/TCS statements, capped at the total TDS/TCS amount.

## 7. How to File Your TDS Return Through the RPU: A Real Walkthrough

This section is a step-by-step walkthrough of an **actual Form 138 filing for Q1 of Tax Year 2026-27** (April–June 2026), prepared on the Protean Return Preparation Utility (RPU) and uploaded to the e-filing portal.

> **Clearing up the most common confusion first:** Form 138 is **not** "Form 24Q under a new name." Form 24Q was the salary TDS return under the Income Tax Act, 1961 and is now used **only** for periods up to 31 March 2026. Form 138 is its replacement under the Income Tax Act, 2025, and applies from 1 April 2026 onwards. If you are filing for April–June 2026, you file a **138** — and the RPU will not let you use a 24Q for that period.

### Before you open the utility

1. **Download the latest RPU version.** This matters more than usual this year. Only a current RPU build carries the new form structures (138, 140, 143, 144) and the Section 392/393 code set. An older build will not offer them.
2. **Confirm all deposits are made.** For Q1, all TDS deducted in June 2026 had to be deposited by **7 July 2026**.
3. **Reconcile challans to your deduction register.** A challan you forgot to enter surfaces later as a short-payment default.
4. **Validate every deductee PAN.** Higher-rate deduction for invalid or missing PAN continues under the new Act.

- - -

### Step 1 — Download the latest RPU

Download the current version of the **Protean Quarterly e-TDS/TCS Return Preparation Utility (RPU)**. It is free software. Do not reuse an older copy sitting on your desktop from last year's filings.

### Step 2 — Select the form and statement type

![RPU form selection screen](images/rpu-step1-form-selection.jpeg)

On the opening screen:

* **Form No.** — select **138** (salary TDS for Tax Year 2026-27).
* **Select type of Statement to be prepared** — choose **Regular** for an original return. Choose **Correction** only if you are revising a return already processed by CPC.
* Click **Click to Continue**.

> Filing a *correction* to a Q4 FY 2025-26 return? That is a different exercise entirely — select Form **24Q**, not 138. The old Act governs that period.

### Step 3 — Fill the Form tab (deductor details)

![RPU Form tab showing Form 138](images/rpu-step2-form-tab.jpeg)

Notice the header on this screen — it is the clearest confirmation you are in the new regime:

> *"Quarterly statement of deduction of tax under section 397(3)(b) of the Act in respect of salary paid to employee under section 392..."*

Section **397(3)(b)** is the filing provision and Section **392** is salary TDS — both under the Income Tax Act, 2025. A 24Q would have cited Section 200(3) and Section 192.

Key fields in **Part A**:

| Field                 | What to enter                                            |
| --------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------- |
| **TAN**               | Your existing TAN. It does not change under the new Act. |
| **PAN**               | Deductor's PAN.                                          |
| **Tax Year** ⭐        | Select **2026-27**. This field is new.                   |
| **Assessment Year**   | Auto-populates.                                          |
| **Type of Deductor**  | Company / Firm / Individual / Government, as applicable. |
| **For Quarter Ended** | Select the quarter — **June** for Q1.                    |

⭐ **The "Tax Year" field is the single most important change on this screen.** Under the old Act you selected an Assessment Year. Under the 2025 Act the concept is Tax Year, and for April–June 2026 it is **Tax Year 2026-27**. Getting this wrong misallocates the entire return.

Then complete **Part 2 — Particulars of Deductor (Employer)**: name, address, PIN, contact number, email. Fields marked with an asterisk are mandatory. Click **Save** as you go.

### Step 4 — Fill the Challan tab

![RPU Challan tab](images/rpu-step3-challan-tab.jpeg)

Move to the **Challan** tab and enter one row per challan deposited during the quarter. For Q1, that is typically three challans — the April, May and June deposits.

Columns to complete for each challan:

| Column                                    | Notes                                                |
| ----------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| **Section Code**                          | ⚠️ Use the **new** code. Salary is **392**, not 192. |
| **Total Tax Deducted**                    | Amount of TDS in that challan.                       |
| **Total Interest**                        | Only if you paid interest for late deposit.          |
| **Total Fee**                             | Only if a late-filing fee was paid.                  |
| **Total Amount Deposited as per challan** | Must **exactly match** the challan.                  |
| **BSR Code**                              | Seven-digit code of the bank branch.                 |
| **Challan Serial No.**                    | From the challan counterfoil / OLTAS.                |
| **Date of deposit**                       | DD/MM/YYYY.                                          |

> **The most common cause of a rejected return:** the amount you type here does not tie to the actual challan, or the BSR code / serial number / date combination does not exist in OLTAS. Cross-check each row against the challan counterfoil before moving on. A mismatch here becomes a short-payment demand notice later.

### Step 5 — Fill Annexure I (deductee details)

![RPU Annexure I deductee details tab](images/rpu-step4-deductee-tab.jpeg)

Move to the **Annexure I (Deductee details)** tab. This is where you map each employee's deduction back to the challan it was deposited under.

Every deductee row must link to a challan row from Step 4 via the **Sr. No.** column. If a challan of ₹11,030 covered two employees, you enter two deductee rows, both pointing to that challan, and the two amounts must sum to ₹11,030.

Illustrative structure (dummy values):

| Row | Sl. No. (Challan link) | BSR Code | Date of Deposit | Challan Serial No. | Total ₹   | Sr. No. | PAN of Employee | Name of Employee |
| --- | ---------------------- | -------- | --------------- | ------------------ | --------- | ------- | --------------- | ---------------- |
| 1   | 1                      | 0000000  | 06/05/2026      | 00001              | 10,000.00 | 1       | ABCDE1234F      | Employee A       |
| 2   | 1                      | 0000000  | 06/05/2026      | 00001              | 10,000.00 | 2       | BCDEF2345G      | Employee B       |
| 3   | 2                      | 0000000  | 07/06/2026      | 00002              | 8,000.00  | 1       | ABCDE1234F      | Employee A       |
| 4   | 2                      | 0000000  | 07/06/2026      | 00002              | 8,000.00  | 2       | BCDEF2345G      | Employee B       |
| 5   | 3                      | 0000000  | 07/07/2026      | 00003              | 6,500.00  | 1       | ABCDE1234F      | Employee A       |

Points that trip people up:

* **The deductee total must reconcile to the challan total.** If they don't tie, the FVU will reject the file at Step 6.
* **PAN must be valid.** An invalid or missing PAN attracts higher-rate deduction and will show as a PAN error in the FVU statistics report.
* **Note the column heading:** it reads *"Permanent Account Number of the employee / specified senior citizen."* The "specified senior citizen" wording reflects Section 393(1), under which certain senior-citizen income is reported on this same form.
* **"Update Mode For Deductee"** (Add / Update / PAN Update) is only used in correction statements. Leave it as-is for a regular return.
* **Use "Insert a Row"** rather than overwriting existing rows.

Click **Save** frequently. The RPU is a desktop Java utility and does not autosave.

### Step 6 — Generate the FVU file

![File Validation Utility](images/rpu-step5-fvu.jpeg)

Click **Create File** in the RPU. It produces an intermediate file, which you then pass through the **File Validation Utility (FVU)**:

1. **TDS/TCS Input File Name with Path** — browse to the file the RPU just created.
2. **Challan Input File Name with Path** — optional. Attach the `.csi` file downloaded from OLTAS to cross-verify your challans against the department's records. **Do this.** It catches challan mismatches now instead of after filing.
3. **Error/Upload & Statistics Report File Path** — choose a folder for the output.
4. Click **Validate**.

**If validation fails,** the FVU writes an error file listing each defect by line number. Fix them in the RPU and re-validate. Common failures: challan-to-deductee totals not tying, invalid PAN, old section code (192 instead of 392), or wrong Tax Year.

**If validation succeeds,** the FVU generates the **.fvu file** — this is what you actually upload. It also produces a statistics report worth reading before you file.

### Step 7 — Upload to the e-filing portal

1. Place the **.fvu file inside a ZIP file**.
2. Log in to the e-filing portal: **https://www.incometax.gov.in/iec/foportal/**
3. Navigate to **e-File → Income Tax Forms → File Income Tax Forms** and select the TDS return upload option.
4. Upload the ZIP.
5. Verify using **DSC or Aadhaar OTP**. On entering the OTP, the return is submitted.
6. The portal generates an **ARN (Acknowledgement Reference Number)**. **Save it.** This is your proof of filing.

- - -

### After filing

* **Issue certificates within 15 days.** Form 131 for non-salary. For salary, the annual **Form 130** (the old Form 16) is due by **15 June 2027** for Tax Year 2026-27.
* **Check the deductee's credit** reflects correctly in their tax statement.
* **If you need to correct something,** file a correction statement — but note the **two-year limit** from the end of the financial year of the original return. After that, TRACES rejects the request outright.

## 8. Penalties, Interest and Late Fees

| Default                             | Consequence                                                                                                    |
| ----------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Late filing of return**           | ₹200 per day under Section 427 (old 234E), until filed. **Capped at the total TDS amount** for that quarter.   |
| **Tax not deducted on time**        | Interest @ **1% per month** from the date it was deductible to the date of actual deduction.                   |
| **Tax deducted but not deposited**  | Interest @ **1.5% per month** from the **date of deduction** (not the due date) to the date of actual deposit. |
| **Return unfiled beyond 1 year**    | Penalty of **₹10,000 to ₹1,00,000** under the Section 271H equivalent.                                         |
| **Failure to deposit deducted tax** | Prosecution possible in serious cases.                                                                         |

**Worked example of the late fee:** you deducted ₹5,000 of TDS on 13 May 2026 and file Q1 on 17 November 2026 instead of 31 July 2026. The delay is 109 days. The fee computes to ₹200 × 109 = ₹21,800 — but since this exceeds the TDS of ₹5,000, the fee is **capped at ₹5,000**.

Note the interest trap: the 1.5% runs from the **date of deduction**, not from the 7th. Depositing a June deduction on 10 July does not cost you three days of interest — it costs you from the day you deducted.

<a name="common-errors"></a>

## 9. The 7 Most Common Filing Errors Right Now

1. **Filing Q1 TY 2026-27 on Form 24Q or 26Q.** The most operationally urgent error. CPC will reject it. Q1 must be Form 138 / 140.
2. **Quoting Section 194C or 194J for a post-1-April-2026 payment.** Triggers system-level validation errors. You will then need a correction statement. In the RPU's Section Code column, salary is **392** — not 192.
3. **Selecting "AY 2026-27" on the challan** instead of "Tax Year 2026-27." Misallocates the payment to FY 2025-26.
4. **Setting a 15 July reminder for the TCS return.** Form 143 is due 31 July, same as TDS.
5. **Issuing a "Form 16" for TY 2026-27 salary.** It must be Form 130.
6. **Using an outdated RPU build.** An old utility will not offer Form 138 or the new section codes at all. Download the current version before you start.
7. **Deductee totals not tying to challan totals.** The FVU rejects the file. Reconcile Annexure I against the Challan tab before validating.

## 10. Frequently Asked Questions

**Q. Is Form 138 just Form 24Q renamed?**
No — and this distinction matters. They are two different forms under two different Acts, and they cover different periods. Form 24Q was the salary TDS return under the Income Tax Act, 1961 and now applies only to periods **up to 31 March 2026**. Form 138 is the salary TDS return under the Income Tax Act, 2025 and applies **from 1 April 2026**. You cannot file April–June 2026 on a 24Q, and you cannot file a Jan–March 2026 correction on a 138. The content is broadly similar; the legal basis and the period are not.

**Q. How do I know, inside the RPU, that I'm on the new form?**
Two tells. The header on the Form tab cites **Section 397(3)(b)** and **Section 392** (the 2025 Act) rather than Section 200(3) and Section 192 (the 1961 Act). And Part A shows a **Tax Year** dropdown, which did not exist on the 24Q.

**Q. My Q4 FY 2025-26 return needs a revision. Which form?**
Old Form 24Q / 26Q, old section numbers, old Act — even though you are filing the correction after 1 April 2026. The governing law is fixed by when the payment or credit happened, not when you file.

**Q. I paid a contractor on 28 March 2026 but deposited the TDS in May 2026. Which Act?**
The **1961 Act**. The triggering event (earlier of credit or payment) occurred before 1 April 2026. The later deposit date does not change the governing law. But note: depositing a March deduction after 30 April attracts interest at 1.5% per month from the date of deduction.

**Q. Do I need a new TAN?**
No. Your existing TAN continues. Only form and section numbers changed.

**Q. Have TDS rates gone up?**
No. Rates and thresholds are unchanged. This is a structural and drafting reform, not a tax reform.

**Q. Is my Section 197 lower-deduction certificate still valid?**
Yes — a certificate issued under Section 197 of the old Act remains valid for payments made on or after 1 April 2026, provided it was issued in respect of projected receivables for Tax Year 2026-27.

**Q. What happened to Form 26AS?**
Form 26A (the accountant's certificate for non-deduction) has been replaced by **Form 149**.

**Q. Can I still correct an old return from FY 2019-20?**
No. The correction window is now **two years** from the end of the financial year of the original return, and the relaxation for older years has lapsed. Reconcile your TDS regularly rather than relying on being able to fix it later.
